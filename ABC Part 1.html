1. What is a formal system?

A formal system is a system where we begin with symbols and rules for changing one arrangement of symbols into another. The rules are meant to be followed exactly. In a formal system, we do not rely on what the symbols “feel like” they mean. We ask: What strings can be produced by following the rules?

Formal systems are important in mathematics because algebra itself can be viewed as rule-based symbol manipulation. When you simplify an expression, solve an equation, or factor a polynomial, you are following rules that say which transformations are allowed.

Formal systems also show up in other areas of inquiry. In logic, they help people study valid arguments. In computer science, they help describe programming languages and algorithms. In linguistics, they can model grammar. In philosophy and mathematics, they have been used to ask deep questions about proof, meaning, and what can or cannot be computed.

2. The ABC Puzzle

In this activity, the only symbols are A, B, and C. You will start with a string that has the same number of As and Bs. Your goal is to turn the whole string into all Cs.

The two rules

  1. Replacement rule: Any AB may be replaced with C. Also, any C may be replaced with AB.
  2. Swap-around-C rule: If a C has a string of symbols immediately before it and a string of the same length immediately after it, those two strings may be swapped.

The string ABBA starts with two As and two Bs, but this route gets stuck at CBA. If you undo the move by changing C back into AB, you are back where you started. This demonstrates why a puzzle can have equal numbers of As and Bs and still not be solvable.
