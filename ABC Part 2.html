ABC Puzzle Activity

Part 2: Computer-assisted practice
What to submit: Use the ABC Puzzle tool to solve at least one puzzle. When you have a solved puzzle on the screen, take a screenshot showing all Cs. Email that screenshot to your instructor. The URL should show which level and starting string you used.

1. Reminder of the rules

  1. Replacement rule: Any AB may be replaced with C. Any C may be replaced with AB.
  2. Swap-around-C rule: Equal-length strings immediately before and after a C may be swapped.

Today’s strings are longer than the paper strings from Part 1. The computer tool makes it easier to try many moves, undo mistakes, and explore patterns.

2. Why use a computer for this?

Computers are especially good at manipulating sequences of symbols. This is part of what happens in many areas of computing: a word processor manipulates text, a compiler manipulates programming-language symbols, a search engine manipulates strings of characters, and a symbolic algebra system manipulates mathematical expressions.

The ABC Puzzle is much simpler than those real applications, but the basic idea is related: we have a string, we have rules, and we want to know what can be reached by applying those rules. A computer lets us test moves quickly and explore a larger “space” of possibilities than we could comfortably explore by hand.

Strategy suggestion: Do not just click randomly. Try to create useful Cs, then use those Cs as pivot points for swaps. If you get stuck, sometimes expanding a C back into AB can open a new path.

4. Optional thinking questions

After you have worked on the puzzles, think about these questions. You do not need to write formal answers unless your instructor asks for them later.

  1. What kinds of starting strings seemed easiest?
  2. What kinds of starting strings seemed to get stuck?
  3. When did it help to turn a C back into AB?
  4. If you were programming the computer to search for a solution automatically, how would it decide which move to try next?
Suggested pacing for one hour: Spend about 10 minutes reading the reminders and side notes. Spend about 45–50 minutes working in the tool. Make sure to leave a few minutes at the end to take and email your screenshot.
